The Last Analog Summer

January 28, 2026 — 9 min read

I found the disposable camera in a desk drawer last October, buried beneath a tangle of charging cables and expired loyalty cards. It was a Kodak FunSaver, the kind you can still buy at a drugstore if you know where to look, and it had been sitting there for more than six years. Twenty-seven exposures, all taken during the summer of 2019. I dropped it off at the last photo lab in my neighborhood, a place that now doubles as a phone repair shop, and waited.

When the prints came back, they arrived in a paper envelope that felt heavier than it should have. The images were grainy, slightly overexposed, the colors shifted toward amber and teal in that way only cheap film produces. There was a shot of my friend Clara standing at the edge of a lake, squinting into the sun. Another of a handwritten sign outside a roadside bar advertising two-dollar tacos. A blurred landscape taken through the window of a moving car. None of them were good photographs in any technical sense. All of them stopped me cold.

The Weight of Paper

That summer, I had taken a month off and driven from Portland to Tucson with no particular itinerary and no data plan. I navigated with a Rand McNally road atlas I had bought at a gas station. I wrote postcards to people I loved, and a few to people I merely liked, pressing the pen hard into the cardstock so the words would hold. I did not document every meal. I did not share my location. When I wanted to remember something, I raised the disposable camera to my eye, heard the mechanical click of the shutter, and felt the tiny resistance of the film advancing. There were only twenty-seven chances to get it right, which meant most moments went unrecorded and had to survive on their own merit, held only by memory.

I did not know, of course, that it was the last summer before everything changed. Nobody did. In retrospect, 2019 has taken on a gilded, almost mythological quality, the final act of a world that still believed in proximity, in crowds, in the casual luxury of showing up unannounced. But I think it felt different even then, even before we had a reason to eulogize it. There was something in the air that year, a collective exhale, as though the century was catching its breath before plunging forward into whatever came next.

We do not mourn the past because it was better. We mourn it because it was finite, and we did not know it at the time.

The Grain of Experience

What strikes me most about those photographs is their texture. Film grain is not noise; it is evidence. Each silver halide crystal that reacted to light on that July afternoon is a physical record of a photon that actually traveled from the sun, bounced off Clara's face, and landed on a strip of cellulose. There is no algorithm between the moment and the image. No computational photography, no machine learning, no optimization. Just chemistry and light and a plastic lens that cost less than a cup of coffee. The resulting pictures are imperfect in ways that feel honest. They show the world not as it should have been, but as it was: slightly out of focus, warm at the edges, and profoundly temporary.

I think about this when I scroll through my phone's camera roll now, those thousands of images shot at sixty frames per second in computational HDR, each one technically flawless and spiritually inert. The modern camera does not capture a moment; it manufactures one. It stitches together multiple exposures, smooths the skin, brightens the shadows, and delivers a version of reality that never existed in any single instant. The photographs are beautiful. They are also, in some essential way, empty. They have no grain because they have no body. They are light without weight.

Nostalgia as Compass, Nostalgia as Cage

I am suspicious of nostalgia, my own most of all. It is seductive to look back at that summer and declare it the last good time, to build a narrative in which the analog world was richer, slower, more real. But that story flatters the teller. The truth is more complicated. I was lonely on parts of that drive. The paper map led me sixty miles in the wrong direction outside of Boise. One of the postcards I sent was to a person I should have called instead, and by the time it arrived, the thing I needed to say had curdled into something I could not unsay. Analog is not inherently virtuous. It is simply a different set of constraints, and constraints are not the same as meaning.

Still, I keep those twenty-seven prints in a shoebox on my bookshelf. I do not digitize them. I do not post them. They exist in one place, and if the box is lost, they are gone. That fragility is the point. It is what makes them precious, the same way a handwritten letter matters more than an email, not because the words are better but because someone sat down and chose, stroke by stroke, to send them. The analog world asked more of us. Whether we gave more in return is the question I cannot answer.

Last week I bought another disposable camera. I have not used it yet. It sits on my desk next to the charging cables, waiting for a moment worth one of its twenty-seven frames. I am not trying to recreate that summer. I know better than that. But I want to remember what it felt like to hold a machine that could not be infinite, and to understand, with each advancing click of the film, that something real and irreversible had just occurred.
